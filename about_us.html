About Us

The Furniture Trades Provident and Benevolent Association (later renamed the Furniture Trades Benevolent Association) was formed in 1903. One of its first actions was to establish a Cottage Village and Orphanage at Cobden Hill, Radlett, Hertfordshire to 'maintain and educate the orphan and necessitous children' of its members. The establishment was formerly opened on 22nd July, 1905, by the Association's President, Samuel James Waring (later to become the 1st Baron Waring) and comprised of two pairs of semi-detached houses. In 1918, a third house was built, and in 1921 the Home relocated to Highgate.

Harold Hunter Ward, who was born in the latter part of the nineteenth century, was an active Freemason and member of Elstree Lodge No.3092 in which he served as Master in 1911 and 1926. When the Home moved out of Radlett, Ward purchased the three properties. A private road, named The Rosewalk, was established between the two nearer to Cobden Hill, and these were developed into two detached houses becoming No.1 and No.2 The Rosewalk. and No.3, situated behind No.1 became the home to a number of Lodges, including Elstree Lodge. Thus, thanks to Ward's foresight, a Masonic meeting place was established in Radlett; the first recorded activity being the consecration of Elstree Chapter on the 25th July 1925.

Following Ward's death in 1940, one of the houses and No.3 was purchsed by Donald Forrester, another enthusiastic Freemason and noted philanthropist. In 1959, Forrester accepted an offer from the resident Lodges, contributing half of the value himself, and the the Masonic Hall (Radlett) Limited company to purchase No.3. Forrester died in 1985 when his generosity was further displaye by bequeathing £300,000 to the Hertfordshire Old Peoples' Establishment as well as other notable bequests.

On the 24th June, 1717, four London based Lodges met at the Goose & Gridiron Ale House in St Paul's Yard and formed the first ever Grand Lodge, electing Anthony Sayer as the first ever Grand Master and so began the formal regularisation of Freemasonry. On Sunday 25th June, 2017, as part of the Tercentenary Celebrations, the Masonic Hall organised an event entitled 'BEHIND CLOSED DOORS' where members of the general public and Freemasons were invited in to view the building. The event was very well attended with xx Masonic and xx non-Masonic visitors.

The Masonic Hall (Radlett) Limited continues to manage the centre to this day through a Board of volunteer Governors. Over the years, the building has been extended and improved a number of times and it now serves over 50 different Lodges and other Masonic units. In addition to the main Temple which is used for regular meetings and ceremonies, there are four rehearsal rooms, a boardroom, lounge bar, and a modern kitchen provides restaurant quality meals with a dining room which comfortably seats up to 70 diners.
