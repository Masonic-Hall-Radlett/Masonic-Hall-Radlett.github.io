About Us

The Furniture Trades Provident and Benevolent Association (later renamed the Furniture Trades Benevolent Association) was formed in 1903. Shortly after its formation, it established a Cottage Village and Orphanage at Cobden Hill, Radlett, Hertfordshire to 'maintain and educate the orphan and necessitous children' of its members. The establishment was officially opened on 22nd July, 1905, by the Association's President, Samuel James Waring, who later to become the 1st Baron Waring, and it comprised of two semi-detached houses. By 1918, a third house had been built but in 1921 the Association relocated to premises in Highgate, London.

Harold Hunter Ward, who was born in the latter part of the nineteenth century, was an active Freemason and member of Elstree Lodge No.3092 in which he served as Master in 1911 and 1926. When the Association relocated to Highgate, Ward purchased the three properties. A private road, named The Rosewalk, was established between the two houses nearer to Cobden Hill, and these were converted into two detached houses which became No.1 and No.2 The Rosewalk. The third house, situated behind No.1, became No.3 The Rosewalk and eventually home to a number of Lodges, including Elstree Lodge. Thus, thanks to Ward's foresight, a Masonic meeting place was established in Radlett; the first recorded activity being the consecration of Elstree Chapter on the 25th July, 1925.

Following Ward's death in 1940, one of the houses and No.3 was purchsed by Donald Forrester, another enthusiastic Freemason and noted philanthropist. In 1959, Forrester accepted an offer from the resident Lodges, contributing half of the value himself, and the the Masonic Hall (Radlett) Limited company was formed to purchase No.3. Forrester died in 1985 when his generosity was further displayed by bequeathing £300,000 to the Hertfordshire Old Peoples' Establishment as well as other notable bequests.

On the 24th June, 1717, four London based Lodges met at the Goose & Gridiron Ale House in St Paul's Yard and formed the first ever Grand Lodge, electing Anthony Sayer as the first ever Grand Master and so began the formal regularisation of Freemasonry. As part of the Tercentenary Celebrations, the Masonic Hall organised an event entitled 'BEHIND CLOSED DOORS' on Sunday 25th June, 2017, where members of the general public and Freemasons were invited to view the building. The event was very well attended with xx Masonic and xx non-Masonic visitors.

The Masonic Hall (Radlett) Limited continues to manage the centre to this day through a Board of volunteer Governors. Over the years, the building has been extended and improved and it now serves over 50 different Lodges and other Masonic units. In addition to the main Temple which is used for regular meetings and ceremonies, there are four rehearsal rooms, a boardroom, lounge bar, and a modern kitchen provides restaurant quality meals with a dining room which comfortably seats up to 70 diners.
